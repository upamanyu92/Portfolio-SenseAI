          What is SIP?

          Systematic Investment Plan lets you invest fixed amounts at regular intervals, averaging your purchase cost over time.

          A Systematic Investment Plan (SIP) is a disciplined method. Instead of a lump sum, you invest a fixed amount monthly in a mutual fund scheme.

          • Rupee-cost averaging: Buy more units when prices are low.
          • Compounding: Returns reinvested, growing corpus over time.
          • Disciplines saving: Auto-debit ensures consistency.

          ₹5,000/month at 12% CAGR for 10 years → ₹11.6 lakh corpus.

          Spreading investments across asset classes reduces total risk without proportionally reducing returns.

          Diversification allocates investments among various instruments to minimise single-asset exposure.

          • Cross-asset: Mix equity, debt, gold & REITs.
          • Cross-sector: Avoid concentration in only IT or PSU.
          • Cross-cap: Balance large-cap stability with mid/small-cap growth.

          A pure IT portfolio carries 2× the volatility of a diversified one.

          Equity Linked Saving Schemes offer tax deductions under Section 80C with the shortest lock-in of 3 years.

          ELSS funds invest ≥80% in equities and qualify for ₹1.5 lakh deduction under Sec 80C.

          • Lock-in: 3 years — shortest among 80C instruments.
          • Returns: Market-linked; historically 12–16% CAGR long-term.
          • LTCG: Gains above ₹1 lakh taxed at 10% after lock-in.

          On ₹1.5 lakh invested, save up to ₹46,800 tax (30% bracket).

          Realised vs unrealised P&L, XIRR, and how to interpret your portfolio’s performance metrics.

          • Unrealised P&L: Paper gains/losses on positions you still hold. Fluctuates daily.
          • Realised P&L: Locked-in gains after you sell. Taxable event.
          • Absolute Return: (Current−Invested) / Invested × 100
          • XIRR: Annualised return accounting for cash-flow timing — more accurate for SIPs.

          XIRR of 12% means your money grew at 12% p.a. compounded.

          Both hold a basket of assets, but ETFs trade live on exchanges while mutual funds are priced at end-of-day NAV.

          Regularly realigning asset weights to target allocation keeps your risk profile intact as markets move.

          • Time-based: Rebalance quarterly or annually.
          • Threshold-based: Rebalance when any asset drifts >5% from target.
          • Tax-smart: Use new inflows first to avoid capital gains triggers.

          Equity grows 60% → 75%? Sell some to restore your 60/40 split.

          Public Sector Undertakings offer dividend income and policy-driven growth, backed by government ownership.

          • Dividend yield: PSUs often pay higher dividends due to policy mandates.
          • Policy tailwinds: Infrastructure thrust can drive strong sector rallies.
          • Risks: Slower decision-making; subject to potential divestment.
          • Examples: NTPC, NHPC, NALCO, SBI, BEL, ONGC.

          Gold ETFs and Sovereign Gold Bonds provide inflation hedge and portfolio insurance with tax advantages.

          • Gold ETF: Backed by physical gold; expense ratio ~0.5%.
          • Gold FoF: Fund of funds investing into Gold ETFs — allows SIP.
          • SGB: RBI-issued bonds with 2.5% annual interest + price appreciation. Tax-free on maturity.
          • Recommended: 5–15% of portfolio for hedging inflation and equity volatility.
